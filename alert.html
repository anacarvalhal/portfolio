Overview

><A global EMR for paper-free healthcare

Alert® is a touchscreen, web-enabled clinical software suite - an Electronic Medical Record with Health Information Exchange, Personal Health Records, Patient Data Management, planning and clinical business intelligence - built to fully computerise healthcare, from entire countries and hospitals down to the individual patient record.

Configured locally for each market and deployed in nationwide, statewide and enterprise-wide projects across more than a dozen countries, it had to work for clinicians at the bedside, in consultations and in the emergency department - fast, touch-first, and safe in high-pressure clinical settings.

My Role

><From product designer to head of product design

I spent eight years at Alert, starting as a product designer and growing into Head of Product Design. As a designer, I designed user interfaces for healthcare software used in paper-free clinical environments, focused on usability, accessibility and the real workflows of clinical staff - designing for touch, for speed, and for safety-critical decisions.

What made the work so formative was the technology itself. Alert was a touchscreen product suite at a time when that was genuinely new - years before smartphones made touch interfaces an everyday gesture. There were no established conventions to lean on, so we got to explore a whole set of new design paradigms from first principles: how to navigate, select, confirm and recover entirely by touch, with controls and targets sized for fingers rather than a mouse, in dense, high-stakes clinical screens.

As the design practice grew, I took on broader responsibility - leading the product design team, setting standards, and embedding user-centred design into how products were built across the company.

Research

><Grounded in real clinical practice

The work was rooted in research. We visited hospitals to see care being delivered first-hand, observing how clinicians actually moved through their day, and ran interviews with the people who would use the software - doctors, nurses and other clinical staff across different specialties and settings.

Internally, this was supported by a conceptualisation department staffed with professionals from across every area of healthcare, who brought deep domain knowledge into the design process and helped us validate that what we were designing reflected real medical practice.

From all of this we systematised the inputs into structured artefacts - use cases, user journey maps, navigation flows and more - turning a complex clinical reality into a shared foundation the whole team could design and build from.

Strategy

><Building the practice, not just the product

As Head of Product Design I led a multidisciplinary team of 29 designers across multiple healthcare products. Beyond the day-to-day, my focus was on the practice itself: setting the design strategy and direction, defining and maintaining the standards and guidelines the team worked to, and keeping quality and consistency aligned across products. A big part of the role was mentoring - supporting designers' growth through regular feedback, reviews and one-to-ones, and helping the team build the skills and judgement to make strong decisions on their own.
